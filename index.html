Ryan Dickey · Mastering Claude AI

Practical Tips & Techniques

Battle-tested methods from Dickey's practical mastery framework — organised into the CRAFT model, 10 power tips, and common mistakes to eliminate.

📖

All tips below are drawn from multiple open source and free resources including self taught Mastering Claude AI: Practical Journey from First Prompts to Pro — the practitioner's handbook used throughout WarsaAi sessions. Free content; Claude subscription required to build.

The CRAFT Framework — Your Prompt Blueprint
C
Context
Who you are, your background, the situation. Claude can't read your mind — front-load the facts.
R
Role
Assign Claude an expert persona. "You are a CFO" unlocks financial depth "write a finance doc" never will.
A
Action
The exact task. Use strong verbs: analyse, draft, list, rewrite, compare, critique, summarise.
F
Format
How you want the output: bullet list, table, numbered steps, JSON, one sentence, 300 words max.
T
Tone
Formal, casual, direct, empathetic, technical, persuasive. Tone shapes everything Claude writes.
10 Power Tips — Dickey's Practitioner Playbook
01

Be Surgically Specific

Replace every vague word before you send. "Write an email" is hopeless. "Write a 180-word cold email to a CFO about reducing payroll software costs" gives Claude a target to hit.

❌ "Write me a proposal"
✅ "Write a 1-page project proposal for a £15k website redesign. Audience: non-technical SME owner. Tone: confident, jargon-free."
02

Always Assign a Role

Opening with "You are a [specific expert]" consistently outperforms plain instructions. The persona unlocks vocabulary, frameworks, and priorities that Claude applies automatically.

✅ "You are a seasoned M&A lawyer. Review this contract clause for hidden risk."
03

Demand Multiple Options

Never accept a single answer when you can have five. Asking for variations forces Claude to explore the solution space — you pick the winner.

✅ "Give me 5 different subject lines for this email, each using a different psychological trigger. Label each one."
04

Set Hard Constraints

Length, scope, and exclusion constraints act like creative guardrails. They make Claude's output tighter, more usable, and closer to your intent in one shot.

✅ "Summarise this report in exactly 5 bullet points. Each bullet must be under 20 words. Do not include figures from before 2023."
05

Request Claude's Reasoning

Add "explain your thinking" or "walk me through your logic" to any analytical task. This surfaces assumptions you can challenge — and teaches you how Claude solved it.

✅ "Which of these two pricing strategies is stronger? Explain your reasoning step by step before giving your verdict."
06

Use Prompt Chaining

Complex work rarely fits one prompt. Break it into stages — Claude's output from step 1 feeds step 2. This unlocks depth no single prompt achieves.

Step 1 → Research brief
Step 2 → "Using the above research, write the executive summary"
Step 3 → "Now critique it as a sceptical board member"
07

Use XML Tags for Complexity

When prompts have multiple moving parts, wrap each element in descriptive XML tags. Claude parses them cleanly and rarely confuses the sections.

<context>I run a property business</context>
<task>Write 3 investment case bullet points</task>
<format>Max 25 words each, investor tone</format>
08

Ask Claude to Write Its Own Prompt

One of Dickey's signature moves: ask Claude "what's the ideal prompt to get X from you?" It surfaces constraints, context, and structure you'd never have thought to specify.

✅ "What prompt should I give you to get the best possible competitive analysis of a fintech startup?"
09

Pre-Load Your Brand Voice

Start conversations by pasting 2–3 examples of your writing. Tell Claude "match this tone and style." It calibrates to your voice faster than any set of adjectives.

✅ "Here are 3 emails I've written. [PASTE]. Now write a follow-up to [NAME] in exactly this voice."
10

Make Claude Self-Critique

After any important output, ask: "Now review your response and identify the 3 weakest points." This forces a quality check before it reaches you — and often produces a stronger second draft.

✅ "Review what you just wrote. What are the 3 things a critical reader would push back on? Then rewrite to address them."
8 Mistakes That Kill Your Claude Results
✕
Treating Claude Like Google

Claude isn't a search engine. Keyword queries return shallow answers. Use full sentences and context.

✓ Write prompts as if briefing a smart human colleague.
✕
Accepting the First Response

First responses are starting points. The magic is in the iteration — push, refine, redirect.

✓ Always follow up with "make this stronger" or a specific critique.
✕
No Output Format Specified

Without a format instruction, Claude guesses. You get prose when you wanted a table, or paragraphs when you needed bullets.

✓ End every prompt with "Format: [table / numbered list / JSON / one sentence]."
✕
Zero Context About You

Claude tailors responses to the persona it perceives. If you give no context, it picks one for you — often wrong.

✓ Open with: "I'm a [role] working on [project] for [audience]."
✕
Giant Single-Shot Prompts

Cramming a 10-step task into one prompt overwhelms the model. Quality degrades as complexity rises.

✓ Break into a chain: 3 focused prompts beats one sprawling one every time.
✕
Ignoring Tone Instructions

Claude defaults to a helpful, slightly formal tone. If your audience needs something different, you must say so explicitly.

✓ "Write this in a punchy, direct tone. Short sentences. No corporate jargon."
✕
Not Building a Prompt Library

Recreating prompts from scratch every time is a tax on your time. Your best prompts should live in a reusable template library.

✓ Save every great prompt. That's what the WarsaAi Prompt Exchange is for.
✕
Forgetting Claude Has No Memory

Each new conversation starts fresh. Claude doesn't remember last session's context — you must re-supply it.

✓ Start important sessions with a context paragraph or use a saved system prompt.
